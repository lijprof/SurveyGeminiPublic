
WHAT ASTHMA IS AND HOW IT AFFECTS BREATHING

Asthma is a chronic respiratory disease characterized by a process where inhaled particles signal the body to make more mucus and release fluids. This causes breathing tubes and nasal passages to swell and clog. The muscles that usually keep the airways open begin to twitch and squeeze, trying to make room for the air. This narrowing and inflammation restricts breathing space and is what some people call an asthma attack or episode. Swollen airways fill with fluid and mucus, and the muscles surrounding the airways tighten (bronchospasm).




In the early stages, a person's breathing may look normal, aside from a slight cough or sniffle. But within minutes or hours, the picture changes, as used air gets trapped inside the air sacs and fresh air can't get in.


WHAT ARE INDOOR ASTHMA TRIGGERS

Pollutant levels inside homes and buildings are often two to five times higher than outside. Asthma and allergies are the most common health conditions linked with indoor pollutants.



Common indoor triggers include:


Dust mites: These thrive in pillows, mattresses, upholstered furniture, stuffed toys, and carpets where they find humidity and human dander (shed flakes of skin). Their droppings, when inhaled, trigger coughing, stuffy nose, itchy eyes, or wheezing.



Mold: Molds reproduce by sending tiny spores into the air that land on a damp surface where they can multiply. Inhaling or touching mold spores may cause sneezing, runny nose, red eyes, coughing, wheezing, or skin rash (dermatitis). Moisture control is the key to mold control.




Cockroaches and Rodent Allergens: Microscopic proteins from pest waste (urine and fecal pellets) and saliva can travel through the air and cause allergy and asthma symptoms when inhaled.


Pet Dander: Tiny protein particles from a pet's urine, saliva, and dander fly into the air when the pet or its cage is handled or cleaned. All furry animals produce dander, including mice, guinea pigs, hamsters, rabbits, and ferrets.




Secondhand Smoke: This is a powerful irritant that can quickly set off coughing and wheezing. It is the second most cited cause of asthma flares, and there is no safe amount.





Irritants: These include chemical odors, strong smells, cleaning products (such as bleach, which is an airway irritant ), hobby products (paints, glues ), and strong scents like perfumes and room deodorizers.


Understanding Indoor Triggers
Indoor asthma triggers are anything inside the home that can bother a child's breathing.

These can include products being used, furniture, carpet, and dust, among other things.

Controlling Dust Mites and Dust
Dust mites thrive wherever there is dust. The following recommendations are aimed at reducing dust and dust mites:

Cleaning Surfaces:

Wipe down walls and furniture.

Use a wet cloth instead of a dry one. A wet cloth effectively picks up the dust instead of just spreading it around.

Bedding:

Wash sheets at least once a week or switch them out to remove built-up dust.

Small Items/Toys:

For small items or toys that tend to collect dust, place them in a Ziploc bag and put them in the freezer for 24 hours to help remove dust mites.

Food and Crumbs:

Do not eat in the bedroom or any areas outside of the kitchen or dining area. This prevents leftover crumbs from attracting rodents.

Managing Food and Pests
Perishable Food: Store perishable food items either in the refrigerator or in a tightly sealed storage box to ensure rodents cannot access them.

Air Quality
Air Filters: If you have questions about better air filters for your home to prevent dust, reach out to your pediatrician for free or helpful resources.

Symptoms: The child may experience the muscle tightening (bronchospasms) around their breathing tubes, leading to a lot of coughing or chest pain after activity.

Treatment and Prevention:

If symptoms occur, the child should receive albuterol.

Preventive Albuterol: Talk to the pediatrician about prescribing pre-exercise albuterol. This involves taking the albuterol pump 15 to 20 minutes before any activity to help prevent symptoms from starting.

Managing Exposure:

Keep Windows Closed: Do not leave windows open, as this allows pollen to enter and settle on furniture.

Cleaning Surfaces: On high pollen days, wipe down walls and furniture with a wet cloth instead of a dry one to effectively pick up the pollen.

After Coming Indoors:

When the child comes in from outside, they must take a shower or change their clothes before getting into bed. This prevents pollen from following them into their safe sleeping zone.

Smoke is a significant trigger that can be encountered outdoors.

Handling Outdoor Smoke Exposure:

If smoke is a known trigger for the child, always carry the albuterol pump when you are outside.

If the child starts coughing or wheezing due to smoke exposure, give them the albuterol pump.

Managing Visitors Who Smoke (Preventing Thirdhand Smoke):

No Smoking Indoors: Smoking is strongly discouraged inside the home.

Protocol for Smoking Visitors: If a visitor who smokes is coming over:

They must only smoke outside.

WHAT IS AN ASTHMA ACTION PLAN

An asthma action plan is essentially a cheat sheet for managing a child's asthma. It helps parents and caregivers know when and how often to take medications, and when it's time to go to the doctor, emergency room, or call 911.

The plan typically has three zones based on symptoms and required treatment:


Green Zone (Doing Well): Medications are taken every single day, no matter what. The albuterol can also be used for 15 minutes before any exercise to prevent any triggers.




Yellow Zone (Caution): Medications are taken at the first cough or cold or tightness in the chest, or any problems sleeping, working, or playing. The yellow zone treatment usually consists of taking albuterol (two puffs every four hours as needed). If the child is in the yellow zone for more than two days, or if you're giving them albuterol often, you should reach out to the child's pediatrician.





Red Zone (Emergency): This is the emergency zone. If the child is not breathing, the medication is not kicking in, or they're visibly turning blue or purple, you should go to urgent care or the hospital. The red zone plan, taken on the way, usually consists of taking two to four puffs every 15 minutes for three treatments.

WHAT ARE QUICK-RELIEF AND DAILY CONTROL MEDICATIONS

Asthma treatment involves two main categories of medications: quick-relief and long-term control.

Quick-Relief (Rescue) Medications


Function: These are used to immediately relax the muscles around the tubes of the lungs so you can go back to breathing like you normally would. They relax twitchy airways so the patient can breathe more easily.




Examples: The albuterol inhaler is a common quick-relief medication.


Use: These are used at the first sign of symptoms or before a known trigger like exercise.


Long-Term Control Medications


Function: This is a medication that must be taken consistently every single day, no matter what, whether feeling good or bad. The purpose is to make sure that the mucus and swelling inside of the breathing tubes does not come back. These medications treat and prevent inflammation, swelling, and bronchospasm.



Examples: An inhaled corticosteroid, such as Flovent Diskus (fluticasone).
ASTHMA CAUSES AND PATHOPHYSIOLOGY

Asthma is characterized by a process where inhaled particles signal the body to make more mucus and release fluids. This causes breathing tubes and nasal passages to swell and clog. The muscles that usually keep the airways open begin to twitch and squeeze, trying to make room for the air. This is sometimes called an asthma attack or episode.



In the early stages of an episode, breathing may look normal, aside from a slight cough or sniffle. Within minutes or hours, the picture changes as used air gets trapped inside the air sacs and fresh air can’t get in. Swollen airways fill with fluid and mucus, restricting breathing space, and the muscles surrounding the airways tighten (bronchospasm).


Allergies are the 6th leading cause of chronic disease in the U.S.. Allergic asthma is the most common form of asthma.


ASTHMA SYMPTOMS

Symptoms of an asthma episode and signs that treatment is needed include:

Coughing, with or without throat clearing or sniffling. The cough may come every few minutes or within seconds. An asthma cough is often worse at night or early morning, and sometimes it is the only symptom. It can be dry or mucus filled.


Wheezing, which is a whistling sound or squeaky sound, especially when breathing out. Wheezing is a sign the asthma episode is becoming dangerous. However, not all people with asthma wheeze during an asthma episode, and some may always sound wheezy.



Breathing becoming rapid and difficult.

Chest tightness, which can feel like something is squeezing or sitting on the chest.

Shortness of breath, feeling breathless, like you can’t catch your breath or breathe deeply enough, or feeling out of shape and constantly tired.

Difficulty talking, where the person with asthma usually uses full sentences when symptoms first show up, but uses fewer and fewer words as the episode gets worse.

Decreased energy level, which may decrease slowly or suddenly. If the person has been running, they may stop, lean forward, and place their hands on their knees while trying to catch their breath.

Each person responds to asthma differently and may display any or all of these symptoms during an asthma episode.

Adults In addition to common symptoms, adults may experience adult-onset asthma symptoms, which first occur in adulthood. Adults may also experience:

Fatigue or a general feeling of low energy, which can be due to the extra effort required to breathe.

Difficulty sleeping, as asthma symptoms can worsen at night, leading to disrupted sleep. If asthma wakes a person up more than twice a month, this is a sign of uncontrolled asthma.


Increased mucus production, which may be coughed up or felt as phlegm in the throat.

Exercise-induced symptoms, where symptoms worsen during or after physical activity. Exercise-induced asthma (EIA), also called exercise-induced bronchoconstriction (EIB), is a condition where the airways narrow during or shortly after exercise.


Teens Teens may experience common symptoms, as well as tiredness or fatigue, excess mucus production, difficulty sleeping, and EIB. The limitations on activities due to asthma can lead to frustration and reduced participation. Carrying inhalers and hospital visits can make teenagers feel different from their peers, potentially leading to feelings of embarrassment, self-consciousness, exclusion, isolation, and frustration.



Babies and Children Most people with asthma are first diagnosed in the first 10 years of life. In infants, toddlers, and preschoolers, diagnosis can be hard because their bronchial tubes are small and narrow. Illnesses, allergens, and irritants can irritate their airways, making them even smaller. Symptoms of asthma in babies and children can vary, and it is hard to monitor symptoms in infants since they cannot communicate how they are feeling. Related symptoms may include:




Fast, shallow breathing (an increased respiratory rate).

Fussiness.

Fatigue or decreased activity.

Bellies move more when inhaling or exhaling.

Nostrils open up when breathing.

Symptoms worse at night.

Problems eating.

Recurrent respiratory infections.

Blue tint to skin and fingernails – a sign of an asthma attack.

Children with eczema, environmental allergies, and food allergies may be at higher risk of developing asthma.

Pregnancy Asthma is a common chronic disease in pregnancy. Symptoms can worsen, improve, or remain unchanged, but pregnant women previously diagnosed with severe asthma are more likely to have worse symptoms. Poorly controlled asthma during pregnancy puts the mother and unborn baby at increased risk for complications, as it may mean they are not getting enough oxygen.




Symptoms by Severity Asthma symptoms are individualized, but the severity levels generally show:


Mild Asthma: Symptoms occur a couple times a week but less than once a day. Symptoms may awaken you at night 1-2 times a month. Lung function is usually normal between episodes but may be slightly reduced. Episodes may last a few days and may affect daily activities.





Moderate Asthma: Symptoms occur daily. Symptoms may awaken you at night more than once a week but not every night. Lung function is reduced, and airflow limitation may be present. Episodes may last several days, occur frequently, and may interfere with daily activities.





Severe Asthma: Symptoms occur throughout the day. Symptoms may awaken you at night frequently. Lung function is significantly reduced, with frequent airflow limitations. Episodes can last for days or weeks and severely limit daily activities.



ASTHMA TRIGGERS AND PREVENTION

Exposure to allergens, irritants, respiratory infections, dry or cold weather, or exercise can cause asthma symptoms to become more noticeable. The first step to controlling asthma is avoiding exposure to allergens and irritants in your environment that set off symptoms.


Common Triggers


Allergens in the air: animal dander, mold, pollen, dust mites, cockroach and rodent allergens.


Irritants in the air: smoke, household chemicals, strong odors, air pollution, including fumes from idling school buses and cars.


Weather-related factors: changes in air temperature and humidity levels.


Activity: exercise.


Other illnesses: rhinitis, sinusitis, gastroesophageal reflux, viral infections.


Emotions: stress, crying, laughing.

Smoking and Secondhand Smoke 21% of people with asthma smoke cigarettes, even though they are a known asthma trigger. Cigarette smoke is the 2nd most cited cause of asthma flares. Smokers with severe asthma have poorer asthma control and more unscheduled healthcare visits.



Secondhand smoke is a powerful irritant that can quickly set off coughing and wheezing. It is a mixture of the smoke given off by the burning end of a cigarette, pipe, or cigar, and the smoke exhaled by smokers. It contains more than 4,000 substances, several of which are known to cause cancer in humans or animals. There is no safe amount of secondhand smoke.






Effects on Children: Secondhand smoke exposure causes 150,000 to 300,000 lower respiratory tract infections every year in children 18 months or younger, resulting in as many as 15,000 hospitalizations per year. Children are particularly vulnerable because they are still developing physically, have higher breathing rates than adults, and have little control over their indoor environments. Children exposed to high doses of secondhand smoke, such as those whose mothers smoke, run the greatest relative risk of experiencing damaging health effects. Children with asthma who breathe secondhand smoke have more severe asthma episodes and lower lung function than children with asthma who are not exposed to smoke. Inhaling secondhand smoke may actually cause asthma in some children. Kids with asthma exposed to secondhand smoke at home are 2X more likely to be hospitalized due to an asthma flare.






Prevention: The best way to eliminate tobacco smoke from the home is not to allow any person to smoke in your home or near windows and doors leading to your home. Benefits to quitting smoking include improved lung function, reduced risk of asthma flare, and ensuring you're not harming others via secondhand smoke.


Indoor Air Pollutants Pollutant levels inside homes and buildings are often two to five times higher than outside. Asthma and allergies are the most common health conditions linked with indoor pollutants. Allergens such as animal dander, dust mites, cockroaches, and mold, and irritants like smoke, chemical odors, and dust can cause symptoms.





Dust Mites: They look like tiny monsters, but thousands could sit on a pinhead. They thrive in pillows, mattresses, upholstered furniture, stuffed toys, and carpets where they have a steady supply of human dander. Their droppings can trigger coughing, stuffy nose, itchy eyes, or wheezing when inhaled. They are likely to be found in schools in carpets, naptime floor mats, pillows, stuffed toys, and upholstered furniture.





Mold: Mold growth can attract cockroaches, dust mites, and other pests. Molds reproduce by sending billions of tiny spores into the air that travel until they land on a damp surface where they can multiply. Inhaling or touching mold spores may cause sneezing, runny nose, red eyes, coughing, wheezing, or skin rash (dermatitis), even among people not allergic to it. Moisture control is the key to mold control.





Pests (Cockroaches, Mice, Rats): Microscopic proteins from pest waste (urine and fecal pellets) and saliva can travel through the air and cause allergy and asthma symptoms when inhaled. Lightweight particles can remain in the air hours after being stirred up.



Pets: Warm-blooded animals like hamsters, birds, rabbits, cats, and dogs can cause allergy and asthma symptoms in sensitive students and teachers. Even pets like lizards and snakes can cause problems if they eat live foods, which produce waste products that decay and provoke symptoms. The tiny protein particles from the animal's urine, saliva, and dander fly into the air when the pet or its cage is handled or cleaned.



Home/School Management Guidance (EXHALE Strategy) The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) recommends using EXHALE strategies to help control a child's asthma. These strategies can help reduce asthma symptoms, prevent asthma attacks, reduce limitations on physical activities, reduce missed days from school, and avoid hospitalizations and ER visits.


Education on Asthma Self-Management: Teach your child about asthma in a way they can understand. Talk with your child's doctor about creating an Asthma Action Plan.



X-tinguishing Smoking and Exposure to Secondhand Smoke: Do not smoke or allow smoking around your child, as it can trigger asthma symptoms.


Home Visits for Trigger Reduction and Asthma Self-Management Education: Reduce asthma triggers at your home, such as mold, dust mites, cockroaches, secondhand smoke, and pet dander.


Achievement of Guidelines-Based Medical Management: Ensure your child takes asthma medications as prescribed. Check to see if your child is using an inhaler correctly or if an attached spacer can help.



Linkages and Coordination of Care Across Settings: Have a copy of your child's Asthma Action Plan at any place your child spends time.


Environmental Policies or Best Practices: Make sure your home and vehicles are smoke-free.

Home Cleaning and Reduction of Triggers To minimize mold growth and dust mite populations, keep indoor humidity levels between 30 and 50 percent.


Kitchen: Repair leaking faucets and pipes to discourage mold growth, rodents, and other pests. Limit clutter under the sink and wipe the area clean monthly to discourage pests and mold growth. Store food, pet food, and bird seed in airtight containers. Never store wet sponges or dishrags under the sink. Replace fragranced and expensive cleaners with nontoxic, fragrance-free, and inexpensive alternatives. Use an exhaust fan when cooking to reduce moisture and odors.






Bathroom: Use an exhaust fan or open a window while showering to remove excess humidity. Wipe the shower walls and tub toys dry after use. Use a mold-proof shower curtain.



Bedroom: Limit upholstered furniture, which is a breeding ground for dust mites. Put pillows and mattresses inside specially designed dust-mite-proof covers. Wash bedding weekly in hot (130°F) water. Vacuum and dust the bedroom once each week, using a HEPA (high efficiency particulate air) filtered vacuum. Avoid carpeting in the bedroom if possible. If allergic to pets, keep them out of the bedroom. Avoid using the bedroom for hobby projects. Whenever possible, use unscented personal hygiene and hair care products, and do not use scented candles or odor-masking room deodorizers. Keep potted plants (a source of mold growth) out of the bedroom.






Laundry Area: Always keep your laundry area clean and dry to eliminate mold, a sign of high air moisture content and possible hidden water damage. Bleach is an airway irritant known to produce symptoms in some adults and children with asthma, so avoid using it. Use unscented laundry products.



Basement/Attic: Do not use the basement for sleeping purposes, and family members with asthma or allergies should minimize time spent in the basement. Use a dehumidifier when humidity climbs over 50 percent. Avoid using carpet on concrete basement floors. Wash mold off hard surfaces and dry completely.

ASTHMA MANAGEMENT GUIDANCE

Asthma management requires a partnership with a healthcare provider and adherence to a personal Asthma Action Plan.

Asthma Action Plan An asthma action plan is essentially a cheat sheet for a child’s asthma, used to know when and how often to take medications, and when it’s time to go to the doctor, emergency room, or call 911.

It typically has three zones:


Green Zone (Doing Well): Medications are taken every single day, no matter what. Daily medications are used. Allergy medications can also be used during the green zone.




Yellow Zone (Caution/Worsening Symptoms): Medications are taken at the first cough or cold or tightness in the chest, or any problems sleeping, working, or playing. The child should do the yellow zone treatment. If a child is in the yellow zone for more than two days, or albuterol is needed often to control their asthma, reach out to the child's pediatrician to ensure the plan is up to date.





Red Zone (Medical Alert/Emergency): Medications are taken on the way to the hospital. This is the emergency zone. If the child is not breathing, the medication is not kicking in, or they are visibly turning blue or purple, go to urgent care or the hospital.



Seeking Medical Care When asthma is uncontrolled or limits daily activity, it's important to speak up and work with the care team.

When to Seek Emergency Assistance (Asthma Attack/Red Zone) Signs that a person needs emergency medical assistance:

Breathing doesn’t become easier within 5 minutes after inhaling the bronchodilator medication.

The skin around the person’s neck, collarbone, and ribs may appear to suck in with each breath, or the stomach may contract.


Skin color may become pale or dusky; lips may lose color or fingernails may look slightly blue; dark circles may form around the eyes.


The person may become very agitated, talking in single words only.

Wheezing is louder and longer. Sometimes, wheezing may disappear altogether if the airways are so clogged with mucus that airflow is not strong enough to produce wheezes.

Symptoms do not respond when you take a quick-relief medication.


It feels like you can’t catch a good deep breath or can’t get the air out of your chest.

You can’t talk except in short phrases.

You have a cough that will not stop, or you simply feel too exhausted to breathe.

Your shoulders tense and raise closer to your ears than normal.

It’s easier to breathe while sitting and leaning forward than when lying down.

You start sweating even though your skin feels clammy and cold.

You experience swelling of your throat, tongue, or limbs.

For pregnant women: reduced fetal movements, difficulty breathing when medicines do not help, headaches, vomiting, or vaginal bleeding.

Steps for an Emergency

Call 911 for emergency assistance.

Call the parents, if the patient is a child.

On the way to the hospital or while waiting for help, do the red zone plan (usually consisting of taking two to four puffs of albuterol every 15 minutes for three treatments, waiting a minute between each puff).

Peak Flow Monitoring Using a peak flow meter can help identify the early warning signs of an asthma attack. A peak flow meter is a handheld device that measures Peak Expiratory Flow Rate (PEFR). PEFR is the maximum speed you can force air out of your lung’s large airways and shows how well your lungs are working.



Self-Management and Lifestyle Patients should take a broader view of their health and consider lifestyle factors, as the lungs work with every organ in the body. Taking a whole-body approach is vital because asthma can be very dangerous if not controlled.




Sleep: Getting enough sleep is key to overall health and immune system control, which plays a huge role in lung health. Adults should aim for eight hours of sleep a night. Poor sleep makes a person more likely to have uncontrolled asthma or allergies and to catch infections or viruses that can affect the lungs.



Avoid exposure to blue light from screens for 1-2 hours before bed, as it suppresses melatonin.

Avoid alcohol or caffeine near bedtime.

Stick to a routine bedtime.

Try not to take naps during the day.


Exercise: Exercise can help keep airway inflammation under control and offers benefits like improved lung function, greater endurance, and improved immune system. If asthma is not well-controlled, exercise can be risky, so clearance from a doctor is important. Starting with low-impact activities like 10-15 minutes of walking is recommended. Strength training with light weights can help build stamina without impacting the lungs.






Nutrition and Weight: Keep your weight within a healthy range, as there are many links between being overweight and having problems with lung health. Following an anti-inflammatory diet (lean meats, fresh fruits and vegetables, limiting carbohydrates and alcohol) can play a valuable role.




Stress and Anxiety: Stress can worsen lung diseases, often triggering a difficult cycle of breathing problems or mimicking asthma symptoms. Controlling stress needs to be a priority. Meditating or yoga can create a calming effect and help immensely with lung health. Asthma and allergy patients with untreated depression should seek counseling.





Environmental Factors: Know your triggers, and adjust medications when air quality may be bad. Climate change is impacting asthma and allergies because warmer temperatures are causing pollen seasons to last longer and plants to produce more potent pollen.


Anaphylaxis Some children with asthma also have anaphylaxis, a life-threatening allergic reaction. The throat, tongue, and lips swell and block breathing passages. It must be treated immediately, usually with auto-injectable epinephrine. The medication wears off after 20 minutes, so the child should be taken immediately to the hospital, and two auto-injectors should be with the child at all times, as a second injection may be needed on the way.



Medication Administration at School Children with asthma need immediate access to prescribed quick-acting inhaled bronchodilators since breathing problems can appear unexpectedly. Oral medications should be stored in the school clinic. Students who have the skills and maturity to carry and use their bronchodilator inhalers do not need to leave the classroom to get them. Restricting a student's access to lifesaving medications is dangerous. Most children with asthma will experience symptoms at school from time to time, and sending a coughing or wheezing student to the clinic wastes precious time and may place the student at risk. Keep the bronchodilator inhaler with the child at all times and store back-up medication in the locked cabinet with the asthma management plan.

Pregnant women previously diagnosed with severe asthma are more likely to have worse asthma symptoms. Poorly controlled asthma puts the pregnant individual at increased risk for complications , which can endanger the health of both the mother and unborn baby.





Symptoms by Severity Symptoms of mild, moderate, and severe asthma can differ, but there may be some overlap because asthma is very individualized.


Mild Asthma: Symptoms occur a couple times a week but less than once a day. Symptoms may awaken you at night 1-2 times a month. Lung function is usually normal between episodes but may be slightly reduced.




Moderate Asthma: Symptoms occur daily. Symptoms may awaken you at night more than once a week but not every night. Lung function is reduced, and airflow limitation may be present.




Severe Asthma: Symptoms occur throughout the day. Symptoms may awaken you at night frequently. Lung function is significantly reduced, with frequent airflow limitations.


ASTHMA ATTACK SYMPTOMS AND EMERGENCY SIGNS

If your symptoms suddenly worsen, you may be having an asthma attack. Any of these signs indicate the need for immediate medical treatment:


Symptoms don't respond when you take a quick-relief medication.

It feels like you can't catch a good deep breath or can't get the air out of your chest.

You can't talk except in short phrases.

You have a cough that will not stop, or you simply feel too exhausted to breathe.

Your shoulders tense and raise closer to your ears than normal.

It's easier to breathe while sitting and leaning forward than when lying down.

Your fingernails turn blue, or your lips become bluish or gray in color.

You start sweating even though your skin feels clammy and cold.

The skin around your chest, ribs, and collarbones sinks in with each breath and you're using stomach muscles to help you breathe.

You experience swelling of your throat, tongue, or limbs.

Wheezing may disappear altogether if the airways are so clogged with mucus that airflow is not strong enough to produce wheezes.

Emergency Signs in Pregnancy Symptoms during pregnancy that indicate your asthma is in the danger zone and you should get immediate medical help or call 9-1-1:

Your breathing does not improve in response to quick-relief medication.

You notice reduced fetal movements.

You have difficulty breathing and your asthma medicines do not help.

Headaches.

Vomiting.

Vaginal bleeding.

Actions for an Attack

Take the person away from any obvious irritants that are making it difficult to breathe (such as animals, smoke or chemical smells).

Use the prescribed inhaled bronchodilator immediately. This medication relaxes twitchy airways so the patient can breathe more easily.

Help the patient drink water to keep airways hydrated.

Allow him or her to rest long enough to recover.

Call 911 for emergency assistance if symptoms don't ease within 5 minutes after inhaling the bronchodilator.

ASTHMA TRIGGERS AND PREVENTION

Common Triggers Asthma symptoms can be set off by exposure to allergens (animal dander, mold, pollen, dust mites, cockroach and rodent allergens), irritants (smoke, household chemicals, strong odors, air pollution, including fumes from idling school buses and cars), activities (exercise), weather-related factors (changes in air temperature and humidity levels), food allergens, other illnesses (rhinitis, sinusitis, gastroesophageal reflux, viral infections), and emotions (stress, crying, laughing).


Humidity and Weather


Humidity in the air is a common asthma x. It contains moisture that makes the air heavier and denser, which makes it harder for people with asthma to breathe efficiently. Humidity combined with hot weather can activate sensory nerve fibers in the airways, causing them to narrow. Humidity also creates an environment where air pollutants and allergens such as pollen and mold linger in the air, potentially leading to a severe attack.




Indoor Pollutants Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) studies show that pollutant levels inside homes and buildings may be two to five times higher indoors than outdoors. Allergens such as animal dander, dust mites, cockroaches, and mold, and irritants such as smoke, chemical odors, and dust often cause symptoms.



The EPA has identified five steps to reduce asthma triggers in schools, which apply to home environments as well:

Clean up mold and control moisture. Moisture control is the key to mold control.



Control cockroach and other pest allergens. Microscopic proteins from pest waste and saliva can travel through the air and cause allergy and asthma symptoms when inhaled.


Remove animal allergens. Tiny protein particles from the animal's urine, saliva, and dander fly into the air when the pet or its cage is handled or cleaned.


Eliminate secondhand smoke exposure. Secondhand smoke is an irritant that can set off asthma symptoms.

Reduce exposure to dust mites. They thrive where they find humidity and human dander, decaying food crumbs, and mold, and are found in carpets, pillows, mattresses, upholstered furniture, and stuffed toys.


ASTHMA MANAGEMENT GUIDANCE

The Asthma Action Plan An asthma action plan is essentially a cheat sheet for a child's asthma, used to know when they should take medications, how often, and when it's time to go to the doctor, emergency room or call 911. The plan has three zones:


Green Zone (Doing Well): Medications are taken every single day, no matter what. Allergy medications can also be used.




Yellow Zone (Caution): Medications are taken at that first cough or cold or tightness in the chest, or any problems sleeping, working, or playing. Treatment usually consists of taking the albuterol either by the inhaler or the nebulizer, usually two puffs every four hours as needed, but consult your pediatrician first. If a child is in the yellow zone for more than two days, or you're having to give them albuterol pretty often, reach out to your child's pediatrician.






Red Zone (Emergency): Take those medications on the way to the hospital. The red zone plan usually consists of taking two to four puffs every 15 minutes for three treatments, waiting a minute in between each puff.



Peak Flow Monitoring Using a peak flow meter can help you identify the early warning signs of an asthma attack. A peak flow meter is a handheld device that measures Peak Expiratory Flow Rate (PEFR). This is the maximum speed you can force air out of your lung's large airways and can show how well your lungs are working.



Working with Your Healthcare Team When your asthma is uncontrolled or limits what you can do in a day, it's important to speak up, work with your team, and get the care you need. If you do not understand your diagnosis, treatment plan, medications, asthma inhaler technique, or anything else, it is very important that you ask your doctor for guidance. Bring your inhaler and demonstrate how you use it to ensure you're using it correctly.

Using Inhaler and Mask Spacer
This device is often used for children.

Understanding the Parts: The mask spacer has three parts: the mask piece that goes over the face, the chamber that slows down the medication, and the nozzle where the inhaler attaches. The inhaler is typically an HFA inhaler.

Preparation:

Shake the inhaler to ensure the medication is mixed.

Insert the inhaler's spout into the spacer nozzle. Ensure you see two "humps" lining up, which indicates correct insertion.

Administration (One Puff):

Place the mask firmly over the child's face.

Press down on the inhaler canister to release one puff of medication.

Watch a flap on the spacer move, or if there is no flap, leave the mask on the child's face for seven breaths. Count slowly (e.g., 1 Mississippi, 2 Mississippi, etc.) to ensure the child receives all the medication.

2. Inhaler and Mouthpiece Spacer
This device is for users who can breathe in through a mouthpiece.

Understanding the Parts: This spacer has no mask; the mouthpiece goes directly into the user's mouth. The inhaler attaches similarly, and you should see both "humps" line up correctly.

Preparation:

Shake the inhaler (it can be done while attached to the spacer).

Breathe out completely before putting the mouthpiece in your mouth. This ensures that only medication, and not air, goes into the lungs.

Administration (First Puff):

Place the mouthpiece in your mouth.

Press down on the inhaler canister and simultaneously take a nice deep breath in.

Note: If you hear a harmonica sound, you are breathing in too fast and need to slow down.

Hold your breath for 10 seconds (count 1 Mississippi, 2 Mississippi, 3 Mississippi, etc.).

Breathe out.

Administration (Second Puff):

Wait for one minute (60 seconds) before taking the second puff. This allows the first puff to settle deep into the lungs.

Repeat the steps: put it back in your mouth, press down, take a deep breath in, and hold your breath for 10 seconds.

3. Using an Ellipta or Diskus Inhaler
This guide is for older users taking a dry powder inhaler.

Important Handling:

Only open the inhaler when you are ready to take the medication. Every time you open it and hear a click, one puff is wasted.

Hold the inhaler like a "hamburger," ensuring your hands do not cover the vents (air holes) on the side. This applies to both the Ellipta and Diskus types.

Administration (One Puff):

Open the device.

Put the mouthpiece in your mouth.

Take a nice deep breath in through the device.

Hold your breath for about 3 seconds (1 Mississippi, 2 Mississippi, 3 Mississippi).

Breathe out.

Finishing:

Close the device immediately after use to ensure it is ready for the next dose.

Lifestyle Management Patients should consider lifestyle factors that can impact their breathing and take a "whole body" approach to lung health.


Good Sleep: Getting enough sleep is key to overall health. When you don't sleep enough, you are more likely to have uncontrolled asthma or allergies, and more likely to catch infections or viruses that can affect your lungs. Adults should aim for eight hours of sleep a night.




Exercise: It can help keep airway inflammation under control. If your asthma is not well-controlled, exercise can be risky, so it's important to get clearance from your doctor before starting a regimen. Benefits include improved lung function, reduced airway inflammation, greater endurance, and a stronger immune system.




Good Nutrition: Following an anti-inflammatory diet (lean meats, fresh fruits and vegetables, limiting carbohydrates and alcohol) can play a valuable role in asthma management. Keep your weight within a healthy range.



Control Stress and Anxiety: Studies show stress can worsen all medical conditions, but it's especially hard on lung diseases, often triggering a difficult cycle of breathing problems. Activities like meditating or yoga can create a calming effect and help with lung health.
